The Legal Outlaw

Dancing in the Street

← Return to Index

In the late eighties I was a young man in my twenties still finding my way in the Law when Iset up my own practice, Nicholas Green Solicitors, in Halifax, West Yorkshire.

To fly solo had never been part of the plan that early in my law career.I had only recently been made a partner in an established law firm,also in Halifax, where I had honed my criminal skills.

The promotion was in recognition of my criminal work heading up the firm’s magistrate’s court practice.The previous incumbent had joined another Halifax solicitors causingmy firm to enforce a non-compete clause in his contract barringany work in the local courts for 18 months. In those bygone days, such clauses were still enforceable and the High Court agreed. He was duly exiled.

The breathing space allowed me to retain and build the criminal work. In short order Isecured a good following [among who?]in the local courts, which is whenI was made a fully-fledged partner.

This was all I had ever worked for. I felt settled and content. I was a married man with a young child. Being a partner was exactly the security I desired.

Unfortunately, it transpired that when the year’s accounts were drawn up, I had earned little more than I had as an assistant solicitor. Given I had paid over £30,000 into the firm, I felt distraught.

My share in the business did not reflect the money earned from the criminal side, as profits were eaten up elsewhere - notably by the ‘in-house’ estate agency run with another local firm. It was worse as, when one partner retired under the partnership agreement, a vast lump sum had to be paid to him. It hit me that others would be retiring in the not-too-distant future, and there was no prospect of my profit share increasing. When I reviewed the partnership agreements, I was unnerved when I realised only mine had a non-compete clause. It seemed I was stuck, and the situation rocked me to my core.

No wonder they had chucked a partnership contract under my nose, I now thought. In my naivety, and with crap accountancy advice from my father’s accountant I had been left exposed.

The truth is, I would never have had the nerve to leave and set up on my own but for my friendship with a probation officer, Christopher Haddock. He had been the author of many bold and daring pre-sentence reports that demonstrated a flair and breadth of vision sufficient for him to be shunned by the prosecution service and court staff.

My co-conspirator was around 10 years older than me and utterly convinced I would succeed as my own boss. Furthermore, he encouraged me to face down the non-compete clause. The reality was financial ruin and unemployment if I was successfully barred from working the Halifax crime beat.Chris brought out the fighter in me. He said I was knackered long-term in any event so I might as well take the plunge.

I was also conscious of becoming a pariah overnight, someone - a Lancastrian to boot - who had shafted an old, well-respected Yorkshire firm. Chris dismissed these concerns and offered to run the office. So I continued working as normal while in complete secrecy Chris locatedan office and looked for staff.

**********

I feel more unnerved in retelling this than I had been at the time, as I was young, fearless, talented and on an adventure.

I recall Chris advising me to start causing antagonisms in the weekly partnership meetings. The loyal dog would begin to poop on the carpet, while ensuring the partners did not become alive to my true intentions to break free and win their crime business.

The day dawned in [month, year?] when I would unfurl the Nicholas Green Solicitors banner, some 400 yards from myoldoffice. I had been to the magistrates’court and transferred the legal aid orders for my casesto the new firm.

Within hours, Halifax was alive with gossip. None of my clients wished to remain with my old firm and, in fact, many others requested that I take over their cases. I almost felt sorry for the old firm when they sent down an aged partner in an attempt to stem the damage. In truth, it was neither his territory nor his decade, and the clients mauled him.

I was still hanging over the precipice, given no amount of early success would mean anything if the non-compete clause was enforced. I had taken my own legal advice and alleged misrepresentation in the partnership agreement. Even though quite positiveabout my defence, it was a fact of life that the judiciary would likely close ranks with the well-respected ‘old school’ firm.

I was shaking the next day when I received a hand delivered letter from the old firm. I feared the worst. But theopening salvo wasin fact a ‘without prejudice’ offer to settle our differences. If I gave up the lump sum I had paid into the firm, then they would not seek to enforce the non-compete clause.In other words I would be free to remain a sole practitioner. The mere fact they had not resorted to a court order suggested to Chrisand I that our opponents were not up for a fight.

At that age, with no capital to my name and a new office to fund, even this outcome was frightening. I considered a counterclaim to claw back a proportion of the partnership lump sum I had paid in, but on Chris’ rapid and sensible advice, decided it was better to settle.

My future was now in my hands and, after all, this is what had spurred me to act in the first place. I had my own practice, with all the responsibilities and pressures that came with it. There was no question I hated failure. Whatever work was required, I would undertake it. Within three months it was clear the money lost were not an issue, Nicholas Green Solicitors was on its way to being very successful. Helpfully, my bank was a staunch supporter of the newly fledged business.

**********

It is against this backdrop that I remember with fondness acting for one Jonathan Quarmby. I was still in the throes of settling my issues with the old firm, dealing with a long list of clients that day, and coping with myriad problems from having my own practice.

Mr Quarmby had spent the night in a cell before being bound over to keep the peace. To agree to be bound over is not a criminal offence, and no legal aid funding applies to it. Therefore, my advocacy should have been done and dusted in seconds.

I was struck by this young man’s humanity. He was a delight, if a tad odd. I decided to take a little time to establish what had led to his arrest. I noticed an aristocratic lilt to Mr Quarmby’s voice as he regaled me with his account. In the previous afternoon’s sunshine, he’d performed a little dance on the stage of the Piece Hall in Halifax. This dance was without music, and without permission, and his detention, it seemed to me, had more to do with his appearance. Mr Quarmby had been sporting a multicoloured leotard which was somewhat worn in the nether regions. It left little to the imagination. Atop his bald scalp sprang a ponytail. To this demeanour he added balletic movements, and I smiled, thinking how tourists must have enjoyed this eccentric’s free show.

Evidently, this was a dance too far for the local official on duty who, when Mr Quarmby continued his ballet, having been requested to desist, jumped on the stage and rugby tackled my client. The official took to his tannoy and called for assistance. Before long the local police attended and handcuffed my Shakespearean fool, carting him off to Halifax police station. I decided I would take a stand for my aristocratic flotsam and jetsam, in that I would not compound his treatment by simply going through the motions. I spoke with the Crown Prosecutor in Court one, where overnight remandeesare dealt with, in the hope of agreeing his release without need for a bind over. After all, Mr Quarmby had been detained in a cell for over 15 hours and had no criminal record.

In those years of advocacy, the prosecutors, save for a few exceptions, lacked any humour let alone compassion in front of Halifax magistrates. They were the types who had been bullied at school and found nurture in freemasonry. Shortly thereafter, Mr Quarmby was brought from the cell below. I informed the court that my client could not agree to be bound over and, given the prosecutor’s stance, the only option was to fix a half-day trial. This surprised the clerk, it being the first instance in the court’s history that an alleged breach of the peace would proceed to trial. No doubt, only I was stupid enough to countenance this when I would receive no fee.

I got to know Mr Quarmby a little better in the intervening period prior to his trial. He was, undeniably, a cannabis user and had little memory of how he had ended up in Halifax. Nicholas Green Solicitors became a drop-in centre where he could chat with staff over a cup of coffee.

Chris, realising a media opportunity, had regaled Yorkshire Television with the Piece Hall dance. The station thought it might lift viewers’ spirits and dispatched a camera crew to Halifax magistrates at around 3pm on the day of trial. Any benefit to my new law firm from thismedia manoeuvre hinged on persuading the magistrates that this lovely, eccentric pothead should be allowed to leave court without a stain on his character.

Chris, as theatrical director, had, unknown to me (but not Yorkshire telly), informed Mr Quarmby to attend the trial in full regalia. Thus, outside court three, ready to testify, was my leotarded client with ponytail intact, returning my smile. It was a masterstroke, as now my defence was alive and well in the confines of the dock.

The case opened, as it was bound to, with the prosecutor, on behalf of the state, working himself into a frenzy. This was followed, flawlessly, with the testimony of ‘jobsworth’, the equally over the top and humourless official. There was no member of the public to corroborate his account of my client’s atrocity. Luckily, the three magistrates assigned that day were fair-minded people. I knew they would, instinctively, realise a hammer was being used to crack (literally) a nut.

Chris had one more surprise in store.As I was cross-examining the local official, who was becoming redder and redder, the chairman of the bench interrupted to suggest I speak to my client. My head turned from stage right, where jobsworth was in full flow, to stage left, where, in the greatest traditions of comedy, Mr Quarmby had begun to re-enact his silent dance.

I could only see one multi-coloured legging and one bare arm above the small wooden facade surrounding the dock. Noone on this occasion looked shocked - in fact, tears were welling up in all our eyes, save for jobsworth and the prosecutor who feigned more animosity. A quick, quiet word from me had Mr Quarmby back behaving impeccably and, shortly thereafter, the magistrates, after a short recess, concluded there was no reason to bind or bend over my client over..

Outside we headedto a grassy knoll overlooking the courthousewhere Yorkshire Television filmed a re-enactment of the dance. Viewers that very night were indeed uplifted over their tea by a short item on Mr Quarmby’s flirt with officialdom in Halifax.

The Queen still ruled, Duncan had not been killed in his bed, and the planets were still aligned in the galaxy. It is proof thatpositive, magical moments can occur if you have entered the legal profession for the right reasons. It also sought to establish my own abilities and ready them for far greater challenges.

Otherwise, all that would have been recorded in the Halifax Magistrates’ register on that day would have been one Mr Jonathan Quarmby having been bound over for one year to the sum of £100.

I, for my part, am pleased to relay the full story and ponder what became of him. Perhaps he now sits as a High Court Judge.
