The Legal Outlaw

De-robed

My criminal practice was booming. Untethered from the confines and strictures of an old, established firm; new horizons were looming.

As word got out, detainees were demanding a call with Nick Green, much to the chagrin of the police. I urgently needed someone who could represent those detained in local police stations at Halifax, Brighouse, Todmorden, Elland and Bradford.

A police station representative is someone who will ensure a client’s rights aren’t violated whilst under detention. It is not a job for the faint of heart, as CID officers see representatives as a nuisance and a hindrance to obtaining full and frank admissions of guilt. Former chief constable of West Yorkshire, Keith Hellawell, belatedly admitted as much in his 2002 biography that getting a confession from someone in detention was all that mattered.

The working hours for representatives were awful given that ‘crooks’ aren’t 9 to 5 types and over the festive period there is always a spike in criminal activity. The murder rate is also at its highest when murderous thoughts, often exacerbated by alcohol, replace wedding vows.

I posted an advert in the Halifax Courier, making it clear previous experience wasn’t necessary. The first person through the door was one Robert Telford, a local man, older than me, who had recently been made redundant from a manufacturing firm after over a decade of faithful service.

I quickly assessed Bob was one of life’s good guys. He knew sod all about the law and, as a man of good character, he had never crossed swords with the West Yorkshire constabulary.

“But you are a quick learner?” I enquired. “I am, Mr Green,” he replied. I paused to take stock. I believed over time that I could forgive Bob’s bouffant, ginger mane. The silence was broken when he insisted there was something he must confess. I envisioned decaying bodies under the patio. “I’m aware of your reputation,” Bob continued: so I need to tell you,”’ He took a deep breath and averted his eyes as he announced: “I admire the police.”

My laughter filled the air, which shocked Bob, as did my outstretched hand. “You’re hired, as you need educating,” I said.

Bob was diligent, respectful and a grafter, albeit a tad timid. But within weeks, I was smiling when I heard him discharging profanities in the reception area after yet another skirmish with the boys in blue. “Still love them, Bob?” I shouted from my office.

Bob’s admiration for the police was not assisted by learning they referred to him as “Nick Green’s bitch”. For my part, I watched a man grow in stature and confidence. I was not the easiest or mildest boss Bob could have encountered in the north of England, but he knew I would not shaft him or permit abuses from the police.

Bob soon saw how the meek, mild and malleable would be trodden underfoot by investigating officers if their representatives allowed it. Cosiness is next to negligence in the criminal arena as coppers wish to turn the police representative into a weapon in their armoury.

In Bob, I had employed an honest and decent man who would never shaft a punter under the pressures of police interrogation. What he lacked in formal qualifications was far outweighed by his belief in due process and the rule of law.

Over time, Bob witnessed the CPS’s (Crown Prosecution Service) hatred for my firm. After all, their instructions came from the police, whose vitriol knew no bounds as I would find out much later down the road.

The ‘independence’ of the CPS is a sad joke. Staff, as these chapters will convey, were happy to lap up all tittle-tattle targeted at my firm. Rather than all of us being on the same road called ‘due process’, albeit on opposing lanes, prosecutors believe their route leads to Damascus and mine to Armageddon.

This vile mindset would inevitably swill into the judicial benches from prosecuting counsel keen to give their Lordships a ‘heads’ up’ to the lawless bastard plying his trade on “their” patch.

I want English law to be fit for purpose and in rude health. I require the law and its construction to be vigorously challenged, for intellect to win over the taking of sides. I do not want the corridors of power to be crammed with invertebrates scratching each other’s spineless backs.

***********

Nicholas Green Solicitors grew year-on-year but with success, came jealousy. The legal profession closed ranks and isolated me as my firm increased its presence not only in Halifax but also in West Yorkshire. New clients abandoned their solicitors and with every request to transfer legal aid to my firm, animosity inevitably grew.

My personality is such that gossip does not interest or entertain me, nor did integration into the corridors of power within the local Law Society. Rumours were swirling that I not only used illegal drugs but peddled them to gain my clientele.

I admit acting for many drug dealers but I also represented burglars and didn’t break and enter houses at night. I also accept my demeanour was of someone agitated, driven by a brain which possessed no brakes, all of which supported the contention that it was more than adrenalin pumping around my veins.

No-one would step out of the shadows so I could address this issue - with a writ, if necessary. My profession prefers stabs in the back, with safety in numbers, all singing from the same hymn sheet. I only recently found out just how widespread these drug rumours were, even finding a stronghold within the CPS, whose Yorkshire headquarters were in Bradford.

Over a decade later, I met Jon Oultram who had joined their ranks in 1988. Qualified as a barrister, he’d spent a number of years working in magistrates’ courts in London. Hailing from the Tadcaster area, he welcomed a return to the north.

Jon is fond of recalling his first day back in Yorkshire. He had been assigned a case in courtroom number one in Keighley, in the belly of the White Rose. He travelled by car through beautiful countryside, a blessed relief from the congested grey shackles of inner London. He entered the courthouse with his briefcase rammed with the day’s files when a middle-aged man with string holding up his trousers approached.

“Are you t’prosecutor?” the man demanded in a gruff local accent. Jon proffered a smile and confirmed his status. “You fuckin’ bastard,” the man replied, then simply walked away. Jon was bewildered by this first encounter, more so when the man never appeared again.

Life in London had been busy for Jon and there had been little time or desire to dwell on office politics. Furthermore, in London courts, one moved about and rarely saw the same faces. There was the cut and thrust of adversarial life, but it rarely degenerated into personal attacks.

On his second day, Jon was to learn that prosecutors in Bradford were not cut from the same cloth. One of the first calls he took was from me demanding disclosure of basic documents for a trial at Halifax Magistrates’ court starting in two days. Jon was prosecuting and found my request reasonable.

Shortly, my office fax machine sprang into action and the material arrived.

“Who are you?” I thought as the fax whirred, assuming a cleaner must have taken the reins of disclosure within the CPS’s Bradford HQ. The animosity and failure to provide disclosure was rife among prosecutors. Exchanges with defence solicitors were always heated, always refused and, in reality, a bore. The CPS was intransigent and non-disclosure had become a matter of principle rather than justice.

Jon much later told me that he came under attack from the prosecution team, who were appalled that he should so willingly, and quickly, have succumbed to their enemy. Jon had not heard of ‘Nick Green’ before this episode, but he certainly had now.

I am pleased to report that our dealings with each other were always professional and courteous. He simply didn’t care for grandstanding and appreciated that, just as he prosecuted, I must defend. We never socialised, our only contact being over the telephone or in the courtroom. When I moved to Salford in Manchester, we lost touch, as I rarely travelled back to Yorkshire. A decade or more passed before I learned he had left the CPS to join a barristers’ set in Manchester.

Much, much later, I met Jon by accident in Manchester Crown Court and started instructing him as it was irrelevant to me that he had been a prosecutor. He’s a good man with a sense of humour and that was all that mattered.

When he learned that I was writing this book, Jon confided in me that the Crown prosecutors in Bradford had slandered me as a dodgy solicitor who took and supplied drugs, some of which were found in the office safe during several police raids.

Jon, by now, used to their personalities, knew humour did not lie beneath these allegations. They were utterly serious. When he asked if I’d been prosecuted locally the incisive question was met with a blank stare followed by the suggestion it must have been dealt with ‘out of area’.

Deceit and falsehoods do not welcome descents into detail. Jon was just astounded that professional men and women would be so cavalier about the supposed criminality of a local solicitor. After all, these prosecutors were supposed to provide quality, independent advice to law enforcers and prosecute cases impartially.

I suppose, as with all lies, I am left to stress that I have never used drugs, and have never, therefore, been found in possession of any. My offices have never been raided by the police, but were often subject to spot audits by accountants sent by the Law Society, my regulator. These became yearly unannounced encounters where I got on with my work in the local courts and left them beavering away in the office for days.

I regret to inform those Bradford Crown prosecutors that nothing untoward was ever found. Given that I was a legal aid practice, my firm held little monies on behalf of clients or corporations. One can only surmise they had been ‘sent’ upon the rumours in West Yorkshire that I was running a drug and money laundering outfit. Again, as with all abuses, I was never told nor established the basis for these interventions. Such activity is based upon whispers in the profession.

As my defence of Michael Robinson had shown, I was not ‘one of them’, so I was to be treated accordingly by every facet of the legal establishment.

*********

Within five years of setting up Nicholas Green Solicitors, firms like mine faced a new existential threat. The government introduced a legal aid franchise system. This meant that firms not approved by the Legal Aid Board were no longer eligible for public funding. I thought my career could be over.

However, I was very fortunate to meet another good man. Simon Pottinger had been a senior official on the Leeds Legal Aid Board before setting up his own business. His firm would, for quite a fee, help firms become ‘franchise compliant’. Simon, being no fool, realised that if he could get the notorious Nick Green as one of his first clients, others would pound down his door. He knew of my reputation but quickly assessed, rather like King Lear, that I was ‘more sinned against than sinning.’

I placed no limitation on his access to every aspect of my practice. Over a three-month period he ensured every existing file was compliant and every new file had systems in place to ensure there would be no failings. A complex and detailed ‘office manual’ had been drawn up, covering all aspects of the business.

Simon, aware that Leeds Legal Aid Board would attempt to make an example of my practice and be over thorough in their due diligence, smiled when he saw who had been assigned to trawl through my offices. A Rottweiler.

After two full days of assessment, which included interviewing staff, Simon was confident we had passed with flying colours. A week later, the head of Leeds Legal Aid Board requested to see me. I took Simon who told me in no uncertain terms to remain calm and not react to what he knew was coming.

I sat in horror as the main man lectured me on my future conduct. I had, indeed, passed, and passed with fantastic marks but, he stressed, he would come under ‘a great amount of flak from the legal profession’ and I ‘was not to let him down’.

Simon kept kicking me under the table afraid I would release a diatribe of abuse, or worse, lunge over the table. I didn’t. I listened but gave no assurances. I was disgusted to be treated like a leper.

Outside, I wanted to issue proceedings to find out what material underpinned the Legal Aid Board’s warning to me. Simon just laughed. He had something else in mind. This turned out to be the lead article in the bi-monthly Leeds Law Society Magazine with my mug on the cover smiling at the West Yorkshire legal establishment.

‘There are more ways to skin a cat, Nick,’ Simon explained. He’d attributed to me a glowing tribute to the integrity of the Leeds Legal Aid Board and the need for a franchise system. It was a PR masterstroke with his firm’s name also in lights as responsible for this outrage. Suffice to say, thereafter Nick became inundated with work.

************

I cite all this as a necessary backdrop to another pivotal moment in my career. I made a complaint to the police about possible corruption concerning a recently retired and well-known officer who’d gone to work for a local Halifax solicitors’ office.

My complaint was made through a new channel that had been opened up between my firm and Halifax police station by an enlightened female chief inspector. She had taken the unusual step of communicating with the enemy to improve case management by ensuring our inevitable clashes could be shorter and less bloody.

In the course of investigating my complaint, the chief inspector established that the recently retired officer had a relative working in her police station who was passing confidential information about arrests that could lead to potential new clients for my rival firm of solicitors.

The serving officer was eventually reprimanded, but this was too much for others in the police station to stomach. They didn’t like that the chief inspector had cooperated with a complaint from the enemy. It was payback time - on me not her.

It’s in our DNA as human beings to be aware we are under attack. Our senses are heightened. In the survival of the fittest, one needs to be one step ahead. In my case, this did not mean being more careful with illegal activities, but I had a real sense of unease. A sense that a storm was brewing. One day, heading past Halifax police headquarters on the dual carriageway towards Bradford, I clocked an officer in an unmarked car who seemed to time his entry from the slip road to be directly behind me. He followed for four miles then pulled me over for travelling at 45 mph in a 40mph zone. The officer was alone, which further concerned me. A speeding ticket was issued and I watched in amazement when he spun his vehicle around and headed back to Halifax.

I reminded myself not to be paranoid, even though, in the back of my mind, I was wondering if the ticket had been issued to link me directly to my now ex-wife’s white Volvo, which I had borrowed as my Range Rover was in the garage being repaired.

The next day, there was another development. At 3:30pm, a member of my staff informed me that the Volvo had been broken into in a car park up from my office. The passenger window was smashed and the radio taken. The Volvo was taken to Halifax police station for fingerprinting.

A week later one of my clients was charged for this and other similar offences. I agreed to keep acting for him as I knew something was amiss. but declared this unusual decision in open court in order to address head-on any conflict of interest. It would allow me access to the case papers which I would otherwise not have seen as a victim of crime.

There were so many questions. Why my Volvo, out of all the 60-odd vehicles parked up? Were the police behind it? Was my client acting on their instruction as an agent provocateur ?

When the case papers arrived I learnt that the incident had been caught on a covert police camera. Apparently, there was an on-going operation to catch car thieves, but this did not ring true.

Days later, I was on my way to HMP Armley in Leeds for a legal visit with two clients. I parked the Range Rover, which had been fixed that morning, and went through security. A guard dressed all in black sat at the front of the small waiting area. I had never seen anyone sit there before which instinctively put me on edge. I suspected he was monitoring my arrival. Shortly, another officer arrived to take us through to the prison yard. The man in black followed directly behind me. Then six more prison officers, also dressed in black, appeared led by the head of security.

While other visitors continued across the yard, I was shuttled off to a side room where the security chief said the prison had received information that I was intending to supply drugs to one of my clients. I wasn’t entitled to know who had given this information, nor its veracity.

It was as if my life was crumbling in front of me. I may have heard rumours but I believed, stupidly, that no sane person could believe I was involved in this unlawful trade. Why had they not used a sniffer dog? Why detain a solicitor when there are less intrusive methods of establishing the presence of drugs? Prison officers could have strip searched the inmate before he was permitted on a legal visit. Once the visit was over, a further intimate search would nail any illegal substance present that could only have been passed by the Blackpudlian.

I was told the prison officers would now strip search me. The ordeal was as close to rape as I wished to encounter. My identity was being raped. I wanted to attack every one of the bastards standing in front of me starting with the head of security. Given there were around eight officers, none below six foot two, this was ill advised. My experience of pressure situations in police stations and courts were no assistance in this wanton act of intimidation.

My mind flashed to where my overcoat and suit jacket had been at my Halifax office. Had I been ‘set up’ by a disgruntled employee? I processed these thoughts because the Head of Security’s voice was so confident, so dismissive.

“Am I free to go?” I asked not unreasonably. I was threatened with a forced strip search if I didn’t cooperate. I considered that my future mental health would not be best aided by the added trauma of being assaulted and restrained. So it was that I de-robed.

Each item handed to the officers, my shoes, socks, shirt, underpants and tie, produced fuck all. Nada. I was staggered when one of the prison officers said my car would be searched. I volleyed back, “You fucking idiot, what’s your allegation now? I forgot to bring the drugs into the prison?”

As I walked to my Range Rover, two police officers appeared which conclusively established the strip search was under a formal Operation by the Halifax police.. To my mind, this demonstrated the pre-planned nature of my de-robing and the certainty that drugs would be found.

I had flashbacks to the recent incidents when I was using my ex-wife’s Volvo. Had drugs been planted in that car to leave me hanging in front of a jury claiming the police had fitted me up?

It’s amazing how much one can process under severe stress. I noted the surprised glances when I pointed out the Range Rover to the two police officers. I belittled the two stooges as they searched the car and found my children’s Buzz Lightyear and Thomas the Tank Engine toys. The policemen searched the boot prompting me to ask if, when nothing was found, my home and office would be next.

‘What’s under there?’ Asked one while pointing at the spare tyre compartment. ‘Ten kilos of heroin,’ I spat back. With that, they slammed the boot without even looking. It would be a real treat if I could now admit 10 kilos of heroin were stashed in the spare tyre. I regret I can’t. What I can say is that I stood in the prison car park certain I was no longer the same lawyer.

As I reversed my Range Rover, it slammed into a car behind. Off I went at speed only to slam the brakes on. Glancing in the driver’s mirror, I saw wild eyes staring back at me. Yet, in those few seconds, I knew that man should have no fear.

I’d come to HMP Armley to conduct lawful business and conduct it, I should. I took a deep breath, collected my files and marched back into the prison. The same prison officers stood aghast as I walked through the security machines setting off alarms. No one stopped me. No one wanted to carry out even a cursory search. I was simply accompanied immediately and in silence across the prison yard to the legal visits room.

Although in no fit state to obtain instructions - I have no recollection of what was discussed - my sole purpose was to demonstrate I would not be intimidated and take a backward step.

Instead, I purposefully sought to conduct the more serious cases beyond West Yorkshire. I was going to take the fight to the authorities, but would do so with intellect and power. Just flailing about wantonly would, for me, not obtain the retribution I was seeking.

I wasn’t stupid enough to believe the intimidation towards me would cease. Or that the negative drugs search would suddenly have the profession take a non-maligned view of me. I was certain, still, to be both a drug user and supplier. In fact, if anything, to those many with ill will, my manic behaviour only increased as a result of the strip search incident. Adrenalin surged through my veins. My brain was on red alert every second of the days that followed.

Equally, I knew this was a fight I couldn’t win. After all, I had R v Robinson as the amuse-bouche. I knew I was in for a Michelin-starred ten-course tasting menu. The first course being ‘deconstructed solicitor on toast.’

**********

As I write, I have just received a psychiatric report on a client serving 30 years for murder. I am dealing with his appeal and, I note, it cites that ‘post-traumatic stress disorder results in heightened fear, anxiety and paranoia that one’s life is in danger. Rendering one liable to act in an increased impulsive, and violent, manner if one perceives oneself as being at risk. An increased propensity to act, without thinking through the consequences of his actions.’

Countless employees could, and would, testify to this applying to me, together with a disregard for the harm my actions caused them. Yet, for some magical reason, in a courtroom I was clinical and in control.

It is not to state I dished out bollockings to people who did not warrant errors being pointed out. I wrongly expected others, with little experience or capacity, to be high achievers, to rise to my level. After all, we were acting for individuals looking at 10 to 30 years imprisonment. I demanded flawless preparation of cases.

I had neither the time, nor patience, to approach fee earners as barristers are called, with a friendly mind-set. It was a regime of fear, and it was wrong. But it dragged me onwards, without the need for counselling. Without the need to address what had happened to me. In reality, I was running away from myself, glad that every waking hour was addressing other people’s troubles.

It’s difficult to write about this even now, to try to make the fact it happened to ‘me’, rather than a third party, centre stage. In so doing, I recollect two instances, within six months of the strip search incident, which exposed that I was not coping.

The first erupted at my brother Howard’s Christmas party in a Blackpool Chinese restaurant. These were not timid affairs. I should also state that I only associate alcohol with sport or restaurants. I am not one to prop up a bar in other circumstances, or even drink at home. I have never been in a bar room brawl, or been banned from any licensed premises.

I have placed before you my mitigation. The night in Blackpool was not helped by the fact I brought three York hockey players to the ‘do’. They’d downed a bottle of single malt in the five minutes before we sat down to eat.

All was going well until I heard on the table behind me a solicitor friend of my by now leathered brother taking the piss out of him to the amusement of others. This triggered my supressed anger, and I volleyed a tirade of abuse on the man. His wife attempted to intervene and received a withering put down which involved references to her facial features. The solicitor, around eight years older and five stone heavier than me, stood up, confronted me and desired fisticuffs. I was back as the man in the strip search room and needed no invitation to hurt someone. I stepped forward, put my head on his and stared into his eyes.

Thank God he backed off, recognising madness as he also conducted criminal work. I don’t wish to suggest I would have won, only that it would have been long and bloody. I had also frightened myself by tapping into such undiluted rage.

Howard, whose relationship with alcohol is explored in greater depth shortly, was too pissed to witness the showdown. But the next day my secretary informed me that he had phoned to impose a two year ban on attending his parties. On reflection, it was one of my crowning achievements, the two years being imposed irrespective of mitigating circumstances. He had no interest or desire to hear from me.

The second occasion again involved alcohol. I was enjoying a meal at a fine dining restaurant near Halifax where I was a regular and always impeccably behaved. I don’t recollect what sparked me off at the end of the meal with a few close friends. My language became colourful and, quite rightly, a guy close by who was having a candlelit dinner with his girlfriend requested I tone it down.

I was once more in free fall spoiling for a fight. I can’t, with certainty, recall the profanities I discharged into the dining room air. But the next day the owner phoned my office to bar me. Apparently I had used the term ‘piss flaps’. I’ve defended most things, but this I couldn’t. Every six months I had my secretary phone the restaurant to see if the ban had been lifted. It never was.
