The Legal Outlaw

Strictly Judged

In the year of our Lord 200X I was acting for a man charged with two robberies. The alleged crimes were serious enough for legal aid to be granted for a QC and junior barrister to represent him. In due course, they advised the client to plead not guilty and a trial was listed for ten days in December at Croydon Crown Court.

The QC had previously dealt with a murder trial of mine and proved himself able and amiable, even though that case ended with the client getting a 30-plus year prison sentence.But ‘results’ ,in the sense of acquittals, have never been my driving force. It is the process that’s sacrosanct to me. If my client has secured a fair trial with all available evidence deployed, then whatever happens next is down tothe twelve members of the jury.

Rob, the junior barrister, was another talent who since being called to the Bar in 2001 had been making a name for himself, in and out of court. He’d become a friend of my wife, Sue, and I’d previously instructed Rob, including one murder where the trial was halted as an abuse of process.

All of which is to say that in the run up to the robbery trial at Croydon both my barristers were ‘up for the fight’ anddealt with all pre-trial issues without any need for me to intervene.

I made my way down south in December merely to ensure the trial went off without any glitches. Unfortunately, the opening coincided with a spate of ferocious snowy weather, which meant court staff and jurors could not use public transport to make their way in.

Luckily, I holed up in a hotel adjacent to the court where I could wait out the weather and catch up with my caseload. After three days the weather relented and it was anticipated the jury would be sworn in the next day.

On the eve of the trial Rob texted asking me to meet him and the QC for a quick conference in a side room at the court. I was there in a flash to find the QC seated and Rob standing by his side like a guardian angel.

Instantly, I sensed the atmosphere matched the ugly, icy streets of Croydon.I sat down facing the QC, who began a little speech, which amounted to this: The defence is crap and the client must be told to plead guilty.

There was no last minute discovery by the prosecution of some damning evidence. So how could my barristers have got their advice and trial preparation so wrong? What had happened to change their minds?

Ignoring the QC, I stared at Rob. ‘Are you in on it too?’ I asked. His voice is usually soft and flaccid, but the tone of his response had the theatrical steel of a voice he would put on much later in his career - as the celebrity TV Judge Rinder. But here, in a real trial without the faux courtroom camp, plain old Rob Rinder, junior barrister, was telling me I should come to heel.

The poor client had spent months on remands thinking there was a chance of getting off and was about to learn that those who prepared his defence thought it was suddenly crap.

The brutality of this legal manoeuvre against me and the client was truly breathtaking. If I’d had a murmur in the weeks leading up to the trial that the QC and Rob would seek to bludgeon a guilty plea I would have brought in other counsel.

Having read this far, it will not come as a surprise that I was having none of their strong-arm tactics. The problem, however, was the QC and Rob knew they held the winning hand. I could not sack them, as that would leave my client conducting his own defence on the eve of his trial. Furthermore, no judge would countenance an adjournment of the trial so I could trawl the barristers’ directory for another pair of invertebrates.

I have been around long enough to know what was likely to be the cause of their volte face and, if I was right, it revealed an ugly and little known truth about how the criminal justice system works.

‘If you have a better paid case to go to – why not just say?’ I said, leaving them in no doubt I thought they were shafting the client.

In their view nothing could be further from the truth. They were acting in the client’s best interests. It was purely accidental both men had woken up that morning, in separate beds, with the sudden realisation the case they’d been handling for months was baseless.

They had even worked out how the client was going to be told to change his plea to guilty! The judge would be asked for a “Goodyear direction”. Put simply, this is an approved device for a defendant’s barrister to draw up a note setting out his client’s culpability which is then sent to the judge requesting an indication of the likely sentence.

The two barristers believed the judge would indicate a term of imprisonment of 3 years or less.I attempted to bring English case law into this ‘going away’ party.

‘Tell meany case law on robberies attracting a sentence of 3 years?’I asked the QC. It was bollocks. An honest appraisal of the crime would attract a minimum of 5 years. But selling that to the client would never fly.

‘I will not be joining you in the cells – I am disgusted at you both,’I said standing up to leave. ‘Congratulations Rob – and no lube.’

The client and I had been rodgered in a legal gang bang and by the look on his face, Rob had much enjoyed the squalid encounter.

So it was that in my absence their plan was executed the next day. But the QC and Rob fell on their arses when the judge made plain the sentence would be nearer five than three years!

The poor client was now doubly fucked. Having been told by his barristers to undress and beg for mercy, now he was being advised to take his chances with a trial by jury overseen by a judge who knew he was guilty.

I never instructed either of them again.

Years later, when I was finishing an early draft of this book, Sue attempted to contact Rob Rinder. She called his chambers and sent a few emails hoping he would step in and save the day for a charity ball in London after the celebrity keynote speaker was taken ill.

By now, Rob was a celebrity himself who had hosted Judge Rinder since 2014 and two years later appeared in sequinned garb on Strictly Come Dancing gliding across the Tower Ballroom in my hometown.

Rob was still a practising criminal barrister who Legal 500 described glowingly as ‘the premier choice for solicitors in cases that demand thorough and complex analysis, sensitive client care and robust advocacy.’

His judicial campery on television had earned some criticism from veterans of bar who felt it ‘denigrated the job and the law.’ I was still of the opinion that his performance during the Croydon robbery trial was far worse than anything Rob had done on TV.

Sue waited for a response, but came there none. Silence. Nada. Fuck all. The man who had earned many thousands of pounds from fiddling with Nick Green’s briefs would not sully himself with a return call or email.

Fate took over as, after watching Manchester City beat Manchester United at the Etihad Stadium, I returned rather pissed to my hotel. Two mates from Essex had come up for the game, so we were in good spirits when we hit the guests-only bar.

Lo and behold, I spied Rob Rinder in a corner with a woman.I did my best ‘Joker’ impression, minus the nurse’s outfit and smudged makeup, sashayed over to the ‘Judge’ and with no preliminary submissions, aggressively enquired, ‘Why don’t you fucking reply when Sue contacts you?’

Gone was the silkiness or steel of his courtroom demeanour. A stutter followed as he attempted a reply to his old Blackpudlian mate. His state of mind unassisted by the 20 stone, six foot frame of Ryan from Essex lurking behind me.

‘I never received her emails’, was the meek response.The bar staff were now alarmed that a non-resident was haranguing their celebrity guest. But the Joker brandished his room key above his head. ‘Ha! Ha!’ I announced loudly, followed by a flash of a grin. And then, travelling from nutter to conformist in the blink of an eye, I held out my arms. ‘I’m only jesting, come and give me a cuddle.’

Moments later, a historic event took place in that Manchester hotel bar. Nick Green was finally embraced by a judge!
