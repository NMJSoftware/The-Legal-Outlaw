The Legal Outlaw

Heads or Tails

← Return to Index

Raymond Martin Marshall is a larger than life character. A man of his word, he is both mentally and physically strong. After being raised in a large family in Manchester, his stomping ground has been in the picturesque town of Newark in Nottinghamshire. Over the years, he had become public enemy number one with the local press keen to relay all his transgressions to its readers. He is from traveller stock.

By the age of 18, he was employing over twenty staff in his pine furniture business. I had been introduced to Raymondo by Kevin Oates, a Halifax client who was mixing with crime families in the Midlands area. Kevin, wrapped up in drugs and guilt, took his life in the late nineties. Little did I realise my dalliances with Ray would shake the foundations of the judiciary in the Midlands. I now relay a number of battles which lead to Chapter ‘X’, being the conclusion of my dealings with him.

Ray was arrested, along with six others, in a major Operation carried out over a 15 month period into the manufacture and supply of Class A and Class B drugs. A warehouse tucked away in the Newark countryside was stated to be the hub of this mini drug empire, which contracted and then supplied illegal drugs onto the streets of Leicester and Nottingham. It was alleged my client had moved from pine sales into the far more lucrative trade in illicit drugs. Ray was cited as the leader of the gang.

The NCS must have raised an eyebrow when his choice of lawyer was a young Halifax one. It was 1989, and I had yet to fledge into a lawyer handling gangland crime on a daily basis.

The police Operation had mainly been covert, and the evidence was rooted in phone data, surveillance and transcripts from probes placed into the gang’s vehicles. The arrests, which also involved the recovery of firearms, hit the headlines in the Midlands. Ray was staring down the barrel of a 12 years’ sentence if convicted of these crimes.

I instructed Manchester counsel, Peter Wright, who was young but talented. His trajectory would lead him into the welcoming, warm bosom of the judiciary, whilst I would become an Outlaw; isolated and shunned but, over time, feared.

The judge, at Leicester Crown Court, appointed to this prosecution had a reputation as a man who meted out long sentences for drug related crimes. I lacked experience in cases which involved a multitude of evidence stemming from a long-standing Operation, but I was a quick learner. I began the process of fronting up the CPS with a catalogue of disclosure requests. My burgeoning belly-button would guide me into the exposed flanks of the opposition.

Where there was resistance or bluster by the Crown, I knew not only to pursue, but to add to my demands. Ray was making allegations of fabrication of evidence and the corruption of the exhibits in the case.

The trial date arrived and the court set aside the first two days to mop up any outstanding matters, including dealing with any legal submissions necessary in the absence of the jury. The truth was these were “market” days, when wheeling and dealing would be underway. Counsel for the defence and prosecution would carve up the case behind the scenes. They would discuss, with the minnow defendants, “special offers” to secure a plea of guilty.

Wright knew better as, with Green and Marshall in tow, such discussions were unnecessary. We had prepared for battle and we were in our armour and ready for bloodshed – whether ours or theirs.

However, as the hours passed and we hit noon on the second day, every other defendant had pleaded guilty! Each of these guilty pleas by Ray’s close associates would be permitted to be placed in front of the jury. The trial judge called Ray into court and, harking to a tome by Agatha Christie, announced,

‘Then there was one, Mr Marshall...then there was one.’

A jury would be sworn in the next day. We descended to see Ray in the cells – he appreciated the trial would now be a sick joke. Five out of the six conspirators had fallen on their swords, acknowledging by their pleas Ray was their leader.

An incredible event then took pace. It was contained in no law book, and certainly absent from any guidance from either the Bar Council or Law Society. Ray asked Mr Wright if he had a coin. Wright brought out a ten-pence piece and, without a pause, Ray called out:

‘Heads for a trial, tails for a guilty plea.’

No sooner had this been conveyed, up the currency went into the air. Ray allowed it to fall on the table. It pirouetted a few times before it came down to rest – tails.

As good as his word, minutes later in the dock Ryamondo pleaded to conspiracy to supply Class B drugs. The Class A was withdrawn, as was the firearms charge, as had been agreed with Crown counsel. To be fair, it was a good outcome, but Ray would be sentenced as the main man.

Wright and I were in awe of Ray. It is one thing having a bit of fun in a minor case, but to fearlessly bet in this fashion, and then follow through, was a special moment in both our careers. It has never been repeated.

‘Why can’t all cases be disposed of in this way, Peter?’ I asked.

‘It’s a lottery at the best of times, Nick.’

The day came when all parties reconvened at Leicester Crown Court for the judge to pass sentence. Ray expected the worst, given the judge’s reputation. It made the tossing of the coin even more outlandish.

The NCS had turned up in force and were clogging up the back of the court. The Press were present, with pens poised, to secure a salacious headline. The judge entered the courtroom. His reddened cheeks and animated gait disclosed an angry member of the judiciary. I looked over at Ray in the dock and we both shook our heads in dismay.

However, it was clear his stare was not aimed at those in the dock, but at the fine NCS officers at the back of the court. The Crown’s QC rose, believing this was his moment, but he was curtly requested to sit down by the judge.

He then proceeded with what can only be described as a rant. He relayed that he had been sitting with his better half, over tea the previous evening, when he witnessed a segment on the local television news. Police were trumpeting the bringing down of the Marshall clan as one of the major arrests in recent years, disrupting the supply of Class A drugs on the streets of the Midlands. In front of their offices, they paraded both large amounts of brick-like drugs and firearms.

With this announcement, the judge slammed his fist on the bench:

‘I will not have my court treated with such contempt!’

His anger stemming from the fact the judiciary do support the boys in blue and CPS in prosecutions, but they do not welcome this being openly touted on the evening telly. It was made worse by the obvious fact the Class A drugs and firearms charges had been withdrawn by the Crown. The coppers should not be the sentencing judge’s ‘warm up’ men, especially if they are not relaying the facts, but a heightened and false mirror of it.

The sentences which followed were lenient, and the faces of the NCS officers were those of men who had lost a loved one. He passed a sentence of 4 years on Raymond! All that was missing was a ten pence piece from the poor box.

Regretfully, the judge, in his haste and anger, had failed to appreciate the police would have recourse to a new legislative power open to the CPS. In the year of Our Lord 1989, the power had been granted for “manifestly lenient” sentences to be passed to the Attorney-General. He would decide whether, on behalf of the State and, hence, the wider public interest, to commence a challenge to the sentences before the Court of Appeal.

Within the 28 day period permitted in law, the Crown duly served a notice confirming they were to utilise the law ‘hot off the press’. Fate dictated it would be no one other than Raymondo subjected to an Attorney-General’s reference.

Wright and I held out little hope the land’s senior judges would not dispatch harsher sentences – keen to demonstrate to the public their new judicial muscles. In fact, at our appearance before the Court of Appeal, it was clear the sentences were to be increased, but the court was unimpressed with the conduct of the CPS. If they had pursued the charges as laid, and not cut a “dirty deal”, then any defendant found guilty would have to been carted off for long sentences. Equally, the deal smelled of a prosecution keen for the defence not to pursue allegations of suppression of evidence and police misconduct. Ray’s sentence was upped by one year. The Lord Chief Justice did not offer to Mr Wright the toss of a coin...
