I am delighted to contribute a foreword to the entertaining and enlightening memoirs of Nicholas Green - a close professional colleague, and an exceptional man.

I first encountered Nick relatively late in his legal career. In 2009, I was asked to take over the representation of a defendant shortly due for trial at Nottingham Crown Court. The client came to see me in my Chambers in London with a young, able solicitor’s representative. They both spoke of “Nick Green” with an edgy reverence, and referred to his views about the case, and how it should be conducted, as though they were being handed to me on tablets of stone.

I bristled; imagining some senior partner, apparently too busy to come to meet me, but seeking to dictate the strategic approach from a distance. It was only some time later that I discovered that distance was far greater than I had imagined - as at that point Nick had retired with his family to the USA and was involved, albeit remotely, only because the client insisted on it.

The retirement did not last.

As time went on, it became clear that Nick was an uncompromising taskmaster, who expected his case workers and his barrister to match his industry, and his unflinching protection of his client’s interests. Those clients marvelled at Nick’s intemperate fearlessness, and were fiercely loyal to him - because they saw his concern was always for them, not for his own reputation or relationship with other lawyers.

Dictating strategic approach is very much Nick’s default setting, and it very seldom involves the path of least resistance. Even so, I quickly learned that it was unwise to reject his creative logic out of hand, and I came to respect his “belly button” - an instinctive indicator which proved surprisingly accurate in diagnosing police misbehaviour.

If you detect in Nick’s writing an increasing frustration and irascibility over time, much of that may be explained by the sense that irreparable damage has been done to our criminal justice system. Delays, underfunding, over-regulation, legislation eroding natural justice, and a judicial preoccupation with efficiency have all conspired to make the task of securing a fair trial for an accused ever more challenging. Nick has never been complacent about these problems - he expresses the frustration that is common to so many criminal lawyers, but so few, it seems, have the stamina to overcome.

Good criminal advocates realise that communication to an audience is most effective and compelling when the advocate has an “authentic voice.” A borrowed anecdote or oratorical device, from speeches made by others we admire, never sounds the same note as the original, and the hollow ring undermines the listener’s trust.

It is not easy to define what makes for an “authentic voice.” The essential components seem to me to be a confident sense of identity, a clear strategic vision, a mastery of the facts, and a directness of delivery. As you will experience, Nick Green has all of these things - and in spades.

Whilst there is always a place for flamboyance and flourish, those refinements of delivery must always be underpinned by substance and well-constructed content - and there is no shortage of content here; some of it quite shocking.

These memoirs are, for the most part, tales from the front line - a daily, career-long fight for something passionately believed in - but also, just as defining, tales of the personal struggle and cost of that fight.

Enjoy reading them - however you respond to them - and I am sure that you will come to appreciate Nick’s unique and direct style, and his wry humour.

Christopher Harding

Barrister
