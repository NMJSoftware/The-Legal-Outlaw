The Legal Outlaw

A Cautionary Celebrity Tale

← Return to Index

My diet of gangland crime rarely served up celebrity fayre. These folk appear in court for road traffic offending (RTAs) or are flushed from the 1970s’ undergrowth for paedophilia crimes.

I left the monotony of RTAs in my distant past and I found paedophilia a cold, unappetising dish. As a newly qualified solicitor, I’d been asked to represent an ex-cleric on a ‘kiddy-fiddling’ charge. Upon taking instructions, in the cell area of Dewsbury Magistrates court, this man of the cloth imparted, in a matter of fact way, the four-year-old complainant had been “asking for it“.

I was hauled before my senior partner to explain why a new entrant into the legal profession had slammed his file in a client’s face? I remained schtum, realising my answer was not within the Law Society rules.

Equally I sensed a resort to the code of Hammurabi’s “eye for an eye” in the Old Testament might see my exodus from this legal firm’s walls. In my silence I kept my job, receiving a verbal warning. I resolved to leave the men of the cloth and DJs to entertain other lawyers. I would move away from knitted cardigans, and into the more tailored dapper arena of organised-crime.

One day Sue informed me I should expect a call from Caroline Flack. Her friend, Mel Sykes, had counselled her that she must seek the advice of Mr G. Caroline had been formally charged with common assault and was captured within the criminal justice system.

I touched base with Chris Harding, as he would be instructed as defence counsel should Caroline be able to break free from the shackles of her current advisors. Calls were made by me to New Zealand to gather human rights implications from Kris Gledhill. Both men were alarmed at the blasts from bugle horns as the Press set off to hunt down their quarry.

We were only gleaning details of the “domestic” from publicity and social media, which we knew from experience would provide but a skewed version of the true events.

Nevertheless, why was Caroline subjected to a ‘non-association’ condition with her boyfriend on her release on bail? This, forbidding any contact by her boyfriend with her, whether by phone, email or in person. An accused citizen is entitled in law to unconditional bail, in circumstances where they are a good character and the offence is of a minor nature. There were no children involved, and Caroline had her own home to return to, independent of the boyfriend.

There was no vulnerable victim, as the 6 foot 7 professional tennis player made clear from the outset he did not support the prosecution mounted by the CPS. The injury sustained amounted to no more than a cut to his head which required no treatment.

I believe it was the invoking of this ‘no contact’ condition which sowed the seeds for her suicide. It ensured she remained isolated and emotional, and it impacted constantly on her fragile mental state.

Chris H agreed that the condition amounted to a punishment, rather than a reasoned route to prevent a further offence. We were both puzzled why a judge had not removed this condition, as it was the right of the defence to appeal its imposition to the Crown Court. Judges are well positioned to ensure restraint and proportionality are observed when the CPS pursue criminal prosecutions.

My maligned view of the judiciary stems from the fact my clientele were target criminals. These appeared in the court system only after the State had mounted complex, lengthy and costly operations. They were not at the front of the queue for leniency and equanimity. The opposite would be true with regard to a mature celebrity of good character appearing on a minor charge, especially when mental health issues were in play.

Kris G, my learned professor, accepted as apposite my assertion this condition trampled upon the boyfriend’s human rights. He was meant to be the victim! But this condition equally damaged him and, by all accounts, affected his mental health. His rights under the Human Rights Convention to freedom of movement, association, expression and the right to a family life could not be impinged.

‘No one will have viewed it from the boyfriend’s angle, Nick. I have taught you well.’

‘They won’t expect this volley,’ I joked.

Kris agreed, should Caroline’s case land within my grasp, to lodge (on behalf of the boyfriend) an application to the High Court to remove the restriction which breached his human rights.

So perverse and corrupted was the imposition of a ‘no contact’ condition in these circumstances, the less than veiled threat was “if” the boyfriend attempted contact it would result in Caroline’s arrest! I would love to see this play out in front of the Press, if the boyfriend had attended and knocked on Caroline’s front door. The sight of the police dragging her into a black Mariah, to halt a conversation taking place in open view, would have exposed the arms of the State so deployed as capricious and beneath contempt.

The British public’s reaction to viewing such footage over their evening meal would have been vocal and damning. The Press would have come to Caroline’s and the boyfriend’s aid, with headlines of “Not in our name” above a picture of Caroline handcuffed in the police van. Absent of these dark arts being deployed by a Blackpudlian, both girlfriend and boyfriend would remain in “check mate” and in fear of the power of the State.

I would request the reader head to the Internet and type in “Domestic Abuse Guidelines for Prosecutors” as this distils the duties of the reviewing lawyer when dealing with these cases. What will become apparent is the absence, in R v Flack, of the warning signs for a prosecution to be mounted. Tucked away under the header “Culpability of the defendant” is a direct reference to mental health considerations:

“Whether the suspect was suffering from any mental or physical ill health before or at the time of the offence taking place“

Wow! How did the reviewing lawyer swerve this direction? In a case where so many other boxes were not ticked?

: No children involved

: Mature woman of good character

: No vulnerable victim

: No past history of domestic abuse

: No actual bodily harm

Into these, a reviewing lawyer should make paramount the mental fragility of an accused, particularly when, in relation to Caroline, there would be a tsunami of coverage by the Press.

The CPS did not have to go hunting for the presence of mental health issues. Her breakdown had been captured on the body cameras of the arresting officers who attended the crime scene. Just as the Crown weaponised the Bail Act, they did the same with the footage.

In the absence of a victim willing to provide sworn evidence at trial, the CPS would place centre stage images demonstrative of her mental disintegration. Uppermost in Caroline’s mind would be the way this would be lapped up by the media. The blood spattered bed sheets were from self-inflicted harm., it was not the blood of the victim. The only person hospitalised was Caroline.

I do not wish to downplay the necessity of addressing domestic violence, nor where necessary to pursue such crimes before a criminal court. These CPS guidelines dovetail into the superb work by agencies, many charitable, who pick up the pieces from vile acts (and omissions) committed by (mainly) men within the household. They are the frontline workers who attend the vulnerable women scarred by their relationships with abusive males.

Yet one size does not “fit all” and the criminal system should not end up abusing a vulnerable law-abiding and mentally unstable citizen. Caroline resolved to finish the prosecution by taking the reins and ending her own life.

I canvassed with Chris the decision by the CPS to level a ‘summary’ only offence. This being one which captures the defendant in the Magistrates court. There is no right to elect a trial by jury.

The reviewing lawyer would have been cognisant of the danger of the prosecution collapsing – either by submissions to a judge or being roundly rejected by a jury. Caroline was much loved by the public, and an able defence lawyer would have exposed the proceedings as a ‘show’ trial – as a cause celebre.

I believed the CPS had wilfully charged ‘common assault’, rather than an offence under S47 Offences against the Person, which permits a citizen to elect a trial by his (or her) peers.

It was an abusive approach and one I would have actively pursued on behalf of Ms Flack. The injury sustained, albeit minor, did meet the statutory criteria of S47, namely an actual bodily harm (ABH).

Upping the ante in this way (as ABH carries a maximum of six years imprisonment, rather than the six months for a common assault), I was likely to hear the cry of learned counsel:

‘Mr Green, Mr Green, Mr Green, are you trying to make your client’s case worse?’

However, if Mr H was instructed, this mantra could be avoided as we both had learnt at the gangland coalface, oftentimes a client’s case must get worse to get better. You read in R v Fury how the introduction by me of 180,000 firearms into a money-laundering case eventually returned Peter to rude health.

It is counter intuitive and only to be deployed by those with fully functioning ‘belly-buttons’. Just as God preached to Moses, prior to his followers finding solace in Egypt, healing crisis is part of the healing process. Keep the faith and reward you will beget. Yet, as a breed, lawyers are meek and seek out shaded areas in which to ply their trade.

Mr H agreed with my bold approach, as he too appreciated a Crown Court judge would not be viewing a custodial sentence as an outcome even if Ms Flack was found guilty.

‘Why would CPS refuse a request by the defence to increase the charge to an ABH?’ Mr H sarcastically proffered.

We both knew CPS would dig their heels in, and by doing so would endorse our submission their charging decision had been abusive. The DNA of abuse applications being the very manipulation of the process present in Ms Flack’s case.

I would have boosted our written submissions by supplying to Mr H a plethora of European case law from our Kiwi Professor, who circles the globe lecturing on the primacy of human rights.

Chris smiled as I advanced my legal stance by asserting CPS would be hung by their own petard. If our abuse was roundly rejected by the Stipendiary Magistrate, there existed a right of appeal to the High Court. The absolute bar, in criminal cases, to proceed to a judicial review only applies to trials on indictment! Those being heard in the Crown Court….whoopsy.

None of this is easy or quick. It is complex, time-consuming and intellectually brave, but critical, as it demonstrates to your lay client the law does permit wrongs to be righted; they are not alone on a life raft in shark infested criminal waters. I am not wishing to suggest those who had Mr G representing them had an easy ride. As Peter Fury and Arran Coghlan can testify, the road was a bumpy one. I am grateful they understood that, to bring down the opposition, I had to remain in battle gear throughout. At times they were shot at by their own lawyer, but it was friendly fire. My brain possesses no “off” switch, and it remains on red alert through all my waking hours. The process is debilitating and exhausting. No client I represented ever felt victory was out of sight. Nor that they were buried under the weight of the criminal justice system.

As I sat on my couch in Lytham, smiling at these legal prestidigitatorial delights, without a call from Caroline it did not amount to a row of beans. It was mere piffery – not sure this is the word. In any event, with her London lawyers accepting the ‘common assault’, and with a trial day having been fixed, it would be a tad late for the Blackpudlian to weave his magic. I was annoyed, as Mel had informed me Caroline was a lovely and caring friend. She, after all, had never met me and knew not a jot of my legal history, save for the removal of Mel’s caution.

I doubted she would be mentally strong enough to cut the umbilical cord with her nominated legal team, who would be in situ from instructions from her management team. It would be a cosy, symbiotic relationship which would not welcome in the presence of a potty mouthed Northern lad, wearing a flat cap with a whippet by his side. I was bound to be asked, age 59, to audition, and it was inevitable it was to the footlights I was headed.

Out of the blue, on the 24th of January 2020, Sue informed me Caroline had been back in touch. She wished to speak with me and apologised for her tardiness. I took the call, and instantly recognised the fear and pain in the celebrity’s voice – it matched that of Ms Sykes. She was close to tears. I was aware Mel had told Caroline not to expect an easy ride with Mr G – there will be little tact.

I broke the ice by interrupting her, ‘Caroline… I must warn you, if you start to cry, I’ll put the phone down.’ I then heard the cackle Caroline displayed on our television screens. Her strength of character was returning.

I must stress I have seen not one page of the papers in R v Flack. What I relay is written from the call. I was not daft enough to believe this would be a private conversation, as at least one of her management team would be silently listening to monitor my advices and report back to HQ.

I should add, when upon asking had she applied to a Crown Court judge to remove the ‘no contact’ condition, she replied ‘I was told not to.’

I spat out ‘Why the hell not, Caroline?!’ as all I was hearing, so far, was the power of the CPS and the vulnerability of Caroline.

‘I was told by my management team and lawyers the Press would turn up. I could not face that again, Nick, after my first appearance. It would just result in more media coverage… And the prosecutor has threatened to play the body cam footage.’

All my fears were being confirmed. All I got back, when I informed her that an appeal would be heard in the judge’s chambers, and she would not have to be present, was ‘this is not what my lawyers have told me.’

The more I heard, the more I disliked. It was no wonder her mental state had taken a nosedive. ‘You never win anything from the back foot – unless you’re a tennis player’, I quipped, garnering another cackle. Her view of the criminal system had been fixed from the first hearing, in which the female prosecutor took an aggressive stance towards Caroline. ‘I am frightened of her, Nick.’ What ?! I bit my tongue, but I could appreciate from Caroline’s standpoint, from the outset, in her mental state she felt overwhelmed and under attack.

Her boyfriend, who attended court to support her and request that the ‘no contact’ condition imposed by the police be removed, was also ignored. If he was not with the ‘good’ guys (CPS) he was against them – he ended up being victimised and the ‘no contact’ condition was endorsed by the Magistrates.

By this juncture I realised, with the trial date fast approaching, there was no chance of Caroline parting company with her legal team. She was many weeks into the case and would not be able to sustain the attacks I would make on her lawyers, management team and the CPS’ reviewing lawyer.

I learnt she had ignored the advice of her legal representative at the police station when she was instructed to remain silent. She had done nothing wrong and so she answered the questions. She was able to point out the movements detailed by her boyfriend, on the day of her arrest, were mostly false.

Our conversation built up to her belief the CPS were likely to drop the case or offer a caution. Her lawyer had obtained more evidence, including from a medical expert, and were hopeful that case would end. ‘It won’t, Caroline’ came my firm reply. My 30 odd years experience with CPS informed me that, once they have dug her heels in, there would be no relenting. In their eyes it would be seen as a capitulation. Moreover, the fear Caroline expressed towards the prosecutor was hardly indicative of a case about to end.

‘You must focus on the trial. It is not far off. You must not get sidelined, Caroline. If it ends, great, but you need to be strong enough to give your evidence. Don’t place all your hopes on it ending before the trial. Stay focused and strong.’

I said this with confidence, but I could see a car crash in the near distance. A criminal team seen to be begging at the feet of the reviewing lawyer is never a pretty sight. It is far too easy, in a domestic violence case, for CPS to justify proceeding to a trial – all the usual mantra is churned out, rooted in an apparent “public interest” which demands retribution by the State, irrespective of the absence of any relevant history of abuse, or support for the prosecution by a non-vulnerable complainant.

‘I want you to take over the case, Nick. I don’t care what it costs. Would do you speak to my manager?’

Oh dear! That avenue had been fruitless in Mel’s case, and I sensed the manager was about to be put on the phone. I interjected, ‘I will speak to your team, Caroline, but face-to-face with you present.’

‘Can you come to London tomorrow, Nick?’

I could not, as I had appointments over the weekend, but I offered to cancel entries in my diary on Monday or Tuesday. Whichever day was convenient, I would catch a train to London.

‘Thank you, Nick, - it has all been blown out of proportion.’

‘I cannot agree more. Nice speaking to you and I look forward to discussing the issues in person next week. Keep strong, Caroline.’

‘I will, Nick, thank you.’

Those were the last words I was to hear from Caroline. She called Sue later that night, to thank her for asking me to speak to her. She informed Sue her spirits had lifted, and she felt more like her old self.

On learning this, I sent a short email which read:

“Nice speaking to you and pleased to hear you’re feeling a little less stressed – trying to sort my diary out re meeting next week – has a trial date been fixed yet? I take it the Crown have yet to serve the Case papers – cheers.”

Around three hours later. I received this:

“I’m just confused… Everyone is telling me different things and I feel like my head is about to explode…It’s all feel so blown out of proportion XX“

I showed Sue the email, and advised her not to book train tickets or a hotel, as it was now glaringly obvious to me she would be staying put with her London outfit. I would not intervene, as the lovely woman did not need a bloody skirmish over ‘who’ would best look after her interests. She was in too fragile a state of mind. ‘Just let Mel know I tried, please.’

A week or so passed, and I was holed up in a hotel in Clitheroe tussling with my “Coghlan” chapter. I was on my own, close to solitary walks for inspiration in the Forest of Bowland.

My phone beeped and I had a message from my mate, Kev, from Essex, which read:

“I see Flack’s dead.“

The shortness and finality of this WhatsApp message shocked me. Kev normally sent me jokes or videos. This was neither. I had the sickening feeling Caroline had taken her own life.

I switched on my iPad and was confronted with “Latest News”, which disclosed Caroline had passed away. I sat at the end of my hotel bed, trying to process this news. I was close to tears. I had not met Caroline, but we had bonded in our telephone call.

I received an email from Mr H who summed up the position:

“INSERT QUOTE”

I then had Sue on the phone in floods of tears. She was blaming herself for not being more robust and ensuring Caroline had sacked her lawyers. ‘Sue – stop it. This has nothing to do with you.’ Yet, in wanton acts of violence, minds do not land on logic.

It was soon reported that our worst fears had been realised. Caroline had hung herself. One can only imagine the terror inflicting her, when she felt compelled to end her mental anguish.

My “Coghlan” chapter was on hold, as my sadness was replaced by anger at those who failed to protect this vulnerable celebrity. Her suicide had occurred the day after Valentine’s Day, when her boyfriend could not communicate with her by any means. It coincided with notification from her lawyers that the CPS would not be dropping the case. It would proceed to trial, and the body cam footage Caroline most feared would be played. My final caution of ‘Keep strong, Caroline’ repeated in my mind.

It will now be for a Coroner to unpick the lead up to Caroline’s suicide. The Coroner has statutory power to make recommendations as to how the criminal justice system could be enhanced to ensure it does act in the ‘public interest’. I have pooled my own thoughts with those of Mr Harding and Mr Gledhill in my desire to flag up stepping off points, and to ensure mental health issues are given more than lip service.

The starting point is to make existing processes more responsive to the circumstances of individual defendants in order to meet the overriding objective of the Criminal Procedure Rules that criminal cases be dealt with justly. These include:

“(2) (a) acquitting the innocent and convicting the guilty

(b) dealing with the prosecution and defence fairly

(c) recognising the rights of a defendant, particularly those under Article 6 of the

ECHR

(d) respecting the interests of witnesses, victims and jurors and keeping them

informed of the process of the case.

(e) dealing with the case efficiently and expeditiously

(f) ensuring the appropriate information is available to the court when bail in

sentence are considered, and

(g) dealing with dealing with the case in ways that take into account:

The gravity of the offence

(ii) The complexity of what is in issue

(iii) the severity of the consequences for the defendant and others affected, and

(iv) the needs of other cases.”

It is not a case of treating “celebrities” in a different manner to other citizens, nor simply accepting their plight and interaction with the criminal justice system is different. Safeguards are warranted to ensure that, broadly, defendants are treated the same. Such safeguards can never be seen as objectionable if the object is to ensure a defendant is treated appropriately and fairly – such that obstacles to this are removed as far as they can be.

The ‘nub’ of the problem is the CPS’ interpretation of what is in the public interest. Under the Crown’s charging code, they must find the public interest as having been met by pursuing a prosecution. What is the ‘public interest’? It is easy to trot out compliance, as the CPS did after Caroline’s suicide, without any true engagement with it.

The way the Press interpret this, along with the media-savvy public, is whether the reported allegations are true, rather than with whether a prosecution is required to promote the welfare/wellbeing of the general public.

Kris Gledhill‘s input then kicks in, as there is little, if any, consideration to the human rights of the accused.  4.14 of the Code for Prosecutors, in dealing with whether there is a public interest, is limited to the suspect’s level of culpability, age and maturity!

How can the Code, which must be human rights’ compliant, fail to ask a reviewing lawyer to engage with how the processing of a prosecution will adversely impact on a suspect? An impact which, as in Flack’s case, was wholly disproportionate to the seriousness of the alleged crime.

Into this absence of detail enters the wholly blunt mechanisms for a defendant’s mental health to be taken seriously. Statute and case law is rooted in the early 20th century, where mental health issues are only kicked into the criminal process at the extremities of mental illness, such as insanity or diminished responsibility. A psychiatric system which believed placing electrodes on a patient’s head would shock him into becoming a rounded human being. Science and medicine has moved forward significantly, but this newfound understanding and acceptance of mental health within society has not been grafted onto the criminal justice system. Without a descent into detail, a reviewing lawyer, as in Flack’s case, can sidestep mental health issues when they do not afford a defence to the charge to be pursued.

The criminal law should provide the police with a strict directive that, in circumstances where a charge would attract significant additional immediate attention on an already ill suspect, this aspect must be addressed with the CPS before a charge is laid. It must not amount to a box-ticking exercise, or one to be rejected solely because it is an offence occurring within the household.

If, as in Flack, there was material demonstrative of mental illness at the time of arrest, this would increase the necessity to engage with such a directive. The body cam footage was not to be seen as a powerful tool for the Crown to deploy in an attempt to secure a conviction. Imagine if the footage have been played to a psychiatrist, along with the history of Caroline Flack. Does anyone believe the expert advice would have been ‘Plough on with the trial.  Expose her to public ridicule. Play the footage. Destroy her career. Deny her access to the one she is in love with…’

All in a case where there was no victim wishing to support the prosecution and the offence, on the scale of domestic violence (both mentally and physically), was minor. If the court system cannot protect a woman of good character in these circumstances, one may as well rip up the guidelines and disavow ECHR as being applicable in domestic violence cases.

Once engaged, the review of mental health must be continuous and monitored, with the courts being alerted from the outset – if a decision is made to charge. It was into this deafening silence that Flack was subjected to a ‘no contact’ condition under the threat of arrest should this be breached – either by her or the boyfriend!

The reviewing lawyer brought to bear upon the unique factors in R v Flack matters patently reserved for cases where there was a history of violence and the victim was so in fear that pressure had been placed on (usually) her to retract her statement. To halt a prosecution in its tracks, after the assignment to it of time, cost and limited resources. I am a defence lawyer, but I applaud the changes in the law which halted prosecutions having to be ended. A healthy system cannot permit intimidation to be rife, whether in gangland crime or violence committed within the confines of a matrimonial home. A fair trial process is subverted by committing such insidious conduct, but as can be seen in the case of Caroline Flack, a reverse position can be subversive and lead to fatal consequences.

The power of the State, and the ‘club’ maintaining its membership without dissent, permitted the CPS to release, after her death, its conclusion that there were “no lessons to be learnt“…

I do hope the coroner will dig a little deeper into these issues, to ensure humanity can breathe within the confines of an adversarial criminal justice system.
